If you're still serving JPEG images on your website in 2026, you're leaving performance on the table. WebP — Google's open image format released in 2010 — delivers 25–35% smaller files than JPEG at equivalent visual quality. That's not a marginal improvement. On a page with 10 product images averaging 200 KB each, switching to WebP saves roughly 500 KB–700 KB of data per page load.

Browser support for WebP crossed 95% globally in 2022 and sits at 96%+ in 2026. The format is supported in every modern browser including Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge, and their mobile counterparts. The argument that "Safari doesn't support WebP" has been wrong since Safari 14 launched in late 2020.

This guide explains what WebP actually is, why it outperforms JPEG, and how to convert your existing image library with no software installation.

## What Is WebP?

WebP is an image format developed by Google based on the VP8 video codec. Google acquired the codec through its acquisition of On2 Technologies in 2010 and released WebP as an open, royalty-free format the same year.

The key technical advantages:

**Lossy compression:** WebP's lossy mode uses predictive coding, where the encoder analyzes surrounding blocks of pixels to predict the values of the current block and only stores the difference (the "residual"). This is fundamentally different from JPEG's DCT-based compression. The result is that WebP achieves smaller files at the same perceptual quality level — or better quality at the same file size.

**Lossless compression:** WebP lossless mode uses spatial prediction and a color transformation to remove redundancy before applying entropy coding. In direct comparisons, lossless WebP produces files roughly 26% smaller than PNG.

**Animation support:** WebP supports animation, making it a competitor to GIF with dramatically better compression ratios. An animated WebP is typically 64% smaller than the equivalent GIF.

**Transparency (alpha channel):** Unlike JPEG, WebP supports transparency. This means WebP can replace PNG for logos, icons, and any image with a transparent background — while being smaller.

### Real-World File Size Comparison

Google's original 2010 research tested WebP against JPEG across a dataset of 900,000 web images. At equivalent SSIM (Structural Similarity Index, a perceptual quality metric), WebP files were 25–34% smaller on average. Independent studies since then have consistently replicated this range.

In practical terms, here's what that looks like:

- A hero image at 200 KB JPEG → approximately 130–150 KB as WebP
- A product photo at 150 KB JPEG → approximately 95–115 KB as WebP
- A blog thumbnail at 60 KB JPEG → approximately 38–45 KB as WebP

Across a site with 50 images, the cumulative saving runs into megabytes per page visit.

## Browser Support in 2026: The Real Picture

The last major holdout for WebP was Safari, which added support in Safari 14 (released with macOS Big Sur in October 2020 and iOS 14). As of 2026, the global browser support for WebP is above 96% according to Can I Use data.

The remaining ~4% of users without WebP support are predominantly using:
- Internet Explorer 11 (effectively dead — Microsoft ended mainstream support in June 2022)
- Very old mobile browsers on outdated operating systems

For the vast majority of sites, serving WebP to all users without a JPEG fallback is the correct decision in 2026. If your analytics show a meaningful proportion of IE11 users, use the `<picture>` element with a JPEG fallback.

```html
<picture>
  <source srcset="image.webp" type="image/webp">
  <img src="image.jpg" alt="Description" width="800" height="600">
</picture>
```

This pattern serves WebP to browsers that support it and JPEG to those that don't. The HTML is slightly more verbose but the performance benefit is worth it.

## When to Keep JPEG

WebP wins in almost every scenario, but there are specific cases where JPEG remains appropriate:

**Open Graph and social sharing images.** Facebook, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, and other social platforms have varying levels of WebP support for preview images. JPEG is universally safe. Check each platform's documentation before switching OG images to WebP.

**Legacy CMS compatibility.** Some older CMS installations or plugins may not serve WebP correctly. If your WordPress installation throws errors with WebP uploads, it's faster to solve the JPEG problem than the CMS problem — for now.

**Photographic archives and source files.** Keep your original source files as high-quality JPEG or raw format. Only convert to WebP for web delivery. You don't want to convert from WebP back to JPEG later — stacking lossy conversions degrades quality.

**Images shared directly via download.** If users are downloading images from your site (photography portfolios, product assets for press), JPEG has broader compatibility in non-web contexts like Photoshop and print workflows.

## Automating WebP Conversion at Scale

For sites with thousands of images, manual conversion isn't practical. Here are the options:

**WordPress plugins:** Plugins like ShortPixel, Imagify, and WebP Express can automatically convert and serve WebP images. These send your images to external servers for processing, so evaluate the privacy implications if you're handling sensitive imagery.

**Build pipeline:** If you're using Next.js, it automatically converts and optimizes images when you use the `<Image>` component. Gatsby and Nuxt have similar built-in image optimization. This is the cleanest solution for statically generated sites.

**Cloudflare Images:** Cloudflare's image transformation service can automatically serve WebP to supported browsers via URL parameters or Polish (their automatic image optimization feature). Available on paid Cloudflare plans.

**CLI tools:** `cwebp` is Google's official command-line WebP encoder. For developers comfortable with the terminal, it's fast and free: `cwebp -q 80 input.jpg -o output.webp`.

For most individuals and small teams, browser-based conversion with a tool like SammaPix covers the majority of use cases without the complexity of build pipeline integration.

## What About AVIF? Is WebP Still Worth It?

AVIF is a newer format with even better compression than WebP — typically 20–30% smaller at equivalent quality. It's technically superior. So should you skip WebP and go straight to AVIF?

The practical answer in 2026 is: use WebP as your baseline, with AVIF where supported. AVIF browser support is at roughly 90% globally, still lower than WebP's 96%+. More importantly, AVIF encoding is significantly slower than WebP — on-the-fly conversion is CPU-intensive.

The `<picture>` element lets you offer AVIF to browsers that support it with WebP as the fallback:

```html
<picture>
  <source srcset="image.avif" type="image/avif">
  <source srcset="image.webp" type="image/webp">
  <img src="image.jpg" alt="Description" width="800" height="600">
</picture>
```

## FAQ

**Is WebP actually better than JPEG visually?**

At the same file size, WebP typically looks better than JPEG — there are fewer blocking artifacts, and the format handles gradients and smooth color transitions more cleanly. At the same visual quality, WebP is 25–35% smaller. Neither statement is absolute — there are edge cases where JPEG handles certain image content marginally better — but WebP wins in the vast majority of real-world web images.

**Can I convert multiple JPEGs to WebP at once?**

Yes. Most browser-based tools including SammaPix support batch conversion. The free plan on SammaPix handles 5 files simultaneously; Pro handles 100. For thousands of files, command-line tools or build pipeline integration is more practical.

**Will converting to WebP hurt my image quality?**

If you're converting from a high-quality JPEG source to WebP at 80% quality, the resulting WebP should look indistinguishable from the original to the human eye in normal viewing conditions. The technical PSNR/SSIM numbers will show a difference, but perceptual quality will be equivalent or better. The mistake to avoid is re-converting from a lossy WebP back to JPEG — each generation of lossy compression stacks the quality loss.

**Does Google prefer WebP images for SEO?**

Google doesn't directly prefer WebP for image indexing — it crawls and indexes both JPEG and WebP images. What matters for SEO is page speed, and WebP improves page speed by reducing transfer sizes. Faster pages perform better in Core Web Vitals, which is a ranking factor. So WebP helps SEO indirectly through performance, not through format preference.
