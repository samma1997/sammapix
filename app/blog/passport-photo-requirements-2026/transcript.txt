Most Common Rejection Reasons

Based on data from passport processing agencies and my own experience, these are the five most frequent reasons photos get rejected.

Number one: Wrong dimensions or aspect ratio. This is the number one rejection reason. A 2 by 2 inch photo is not the same as a 35 by 45 millimeter photo. Many people crop their photo to roughly the right shape but miss the exact pixel dimensions. For the US, you need exactly 600 by 600 pixels at 300 DPI. For a 35 by 45 millimeter photo, you need 413 by 531 pixels at 300 DPI.

Number two: Shadows on face or background. Even subtle shadows behind your head or under your chin will get flagged. This happens when you stand too close to the wall or use a single overhead light source. The fix: stand about 50 centimeters away from the wall and face a large window for even, diffused lighting.

Number three: Eyes not fully visible. Glasses glare, hair covering one eye, or half-closed eyes. Since 2021, most countries recommend removing glasses entirely for passport photos. If you must wear glasses for medical reasons, ensure there is zero glare on the lenses.

Number four: Incorrect background color. Off-white, cream, or yellowish backgrounds get rejected. Your wall might look white to your eyes, but your camera's white balance can introduce a warm tint. Check your photo on a calibrated screen. The background should be pure white.

Number five: File size exceeds maximum. Digital submissions through government portals often have strict file size limits. Singapore requires under 60 kilobytes. The US requires under 240 kilobytes. India requires between 10 and 300 kilobytes. A typical smartphone photo is 3 to 8 megabytes, so you need to compress it by over 90 percent to meet these limits.

How to Take a Perfect Passport Photo at Home

You don't need a professional photographer or a photo booth. Here's the step-by-step process I use to take passport photos at home that pass on the first try.

Step 1: Find a plain white wall. A flat, matte-finish white wall works best. Avoid textured walls, wallpaper, or doors with panels. If your walls are off-white, tape a large sheet of white poster board behind you.

Step 2: Use natural light. Face a large window. The best time is mid-morning or late afternoon when sunlight is diffused. Avoid direct sunlight because it creates harsh shadows.

Step 3: Position yourself correctly. Stand about 50 centimeters in front of the wall to avoid casting a shadow on it. Have someone hold the camera at eye level, about 1.5 meters away. If using a selfie setup, use the rear camera with a timer for better quality.

Step 4: Get the expression right. Neutral expression, mouth closed, both eyes open, looking directly at the camera. Most countries do not accept smiling photos. Remove glasses, headphones, and any head coverings unless for religious purposes.

Step 5: Take multiple shots. Take at least 10 photos. Review them on a larger screen and check for shadows, closed eyes, and off-center positioning. Pick the best one.

Step 6: Crop and resize to exact specifications. Use a crop tool to crop your photo to the correct aspect ratio, then resize it to the exact pixel dimensions required by your country.

Step 7: Compress to required file size. Most government portals have strict file size limits. Use a compression tool to bring your photo down to the required size, whether that's 240 kilobytes for the US, 60 kilobytes for Singapore, or 100 kilobytes for a visa application.

Digital vs Physical: File Size Requirements

There's a big difference between photos for printed passport applications and photos for online digital submissions.

For physical printed photos: print at 300 DPI on photo paper. File size doesn't matter, only print quality. Use the highest quality JPEG at 90 to 100 percent.

For digital online submissions: there are strict file size limits ranging from 60 kilobytes to 10 megabytes. Specific pixel dimensions are required. JPEG format is almost always required. You must compress to meet the size limit while keeping clarity. Some portals reject if the file is too small. India has a minimum of 10 kilobytes.

The smart approach to meeting digital requirements is: crop first to remove everything outside the required frame, resize to the exact required pixel dimensions, and compress last to hit the file size target. This order matters because cropping and resizing first removes most of the excess data, so the final compression step only needs to reduce the file by 20 to 40 percent instead of 95 percent.

Tips by Region

Asia has the strictest file size limits. Singapore requires under 60 kilobytes, China requires 40 to 120 kilobytes, and South Korea caps at 700 kilobytes. India has both a minimum and maximum, which means you can't just over-compress.

Europe is more relaxed on file sizes but strict about backgrounds. Germany requires specifically light grey, France accepts light grey or light blue, and the UK accepts both white and light grey.

In the Americas, the US uses a unique 2 by 2 inch square format, while Canada uses 50 by 70 millimeters. Mexico follows the European 35 by 45 millimeter standard.

The Middle East follows the 35 by 45 millimeter standard with white backgrounds. The UAE has a 200 kilobyte file size limit.

Southeast Asia has unique requirements. Indonesia stands out with colored backgrounds: red for the last three passport pages, blue for earlier pages. Singapore's 60 kilobyte limit is the strictest in the world.

Frequently Asked Questions

What file format should passport photos be in? JPEG is the universally accepted format. Some portals also accept PNG. Avoid WebP, HEIC, or AVIF because government systems typically don't support modern formats.
